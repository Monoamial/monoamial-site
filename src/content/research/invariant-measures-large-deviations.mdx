We study a flow $\phi_t$ on a compact manifold $M$ with a unique invariant
measure $\mu$, and ask how empirical averages concentrate. The central object
is the **rate function** $I$ governing

$$
\mathbb{P}\!\left( \tfrac{1}{T}\int_0^T \delta_{\phi_t x}\,dt \approx \nu \right)
\asymp e^{-T\, I(\nu)}, \qquad T \to \infty.
$$

## A geometric form of the rate function

When the perturbation is gradient-like, $I$ admits a clean variational form,

$$
I(\nu) = \sup_{u \in C^1(M)} \int_M \left( \mathcal{L}u - \tfrac{1}{2}\,\Gamma(u,u) \right) d\nu,
$$

where $\Gamma$ is the *carré du champ* operator. The geometry enters through
$\Gamma$: curvature lower bounds translate, via Bakry–Émery, into quantitative
concentration.

> The slogan: **curvature controls rare events.**

This note collects the estimates I'm currently trying to make uniform in the
perturbation strength $\varepsilon$.
