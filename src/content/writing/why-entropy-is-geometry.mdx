---
title: "Why entropy is secretly geometry"
description: "An expository note on relative entropy as a squared distance, and what that buys you."
date: 2026-03-18
tags: ["entropy", "information-geometry", "exposition"]
---

Relative entropy looks like an asymmetric, unfamiliar object,

$$
D(p \,\|\, q) = \sum_x p(x) \log \frac{p(x)}{q(x)},
$$

but to second order it is just a **squared distance**. Expanding around
$q = p$ with $p = q + \delta$,

$$
D(p \,\|\, q) = \tfrac{1}{2} \sum_x \frac{\delta(x)^2}{p(x)} + O(\delta^3)
            = \tfrac{1}{2}\, \delta^\top G(p)\, \delta + \cdots,
$$

where $G(p)$ is the **Fisher information metric**. So the space of probability
distributions is a Riemannian manifold, and entropy measures (infinitesimal)
geodesic distance on it.

Once you see this, a lot of inequalities stop being magic: Pinsker, the
data-processing inequality, and the concavity of entropy all become statements
about a curved space. That reframing is the whole reason information geometry is
useful to a physicist.
